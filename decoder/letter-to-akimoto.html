Letter to Akimoto

WND Volume 1 · Read on Nichiren Library →
FaithCourageCorrect TeachingPerseveranceWisdom

Background

Written To

Akimoto Tarō Hyōe-no-jō, a lay believer who lived in Shimōsa Province and likely converted during Nichiren's hundred-day lectures around 1260. He may have been related to Toki Jōnin and was friends with other local believers.

When

January 27, 1280, during Nichiren's exile at Mount Minobu. Written three months after the Atsuhara Persecution, during an exceptionally harsh winter with deep snow and food shortages.

Why It Was Written

Akimoto had sent thirty vessels and sixty plates to Nichiren during this period of extreme hardship. The Mongols were preparing for a second invasion, creating national anxiety, and Nichiren was living in isolation on the mountain.

Significance

This letter contains one of Nichiren's most full explanations of proper faith using the 'four faults of vessels' metaphor, and his teaching on the three principles regarding slanderers-individual, family, and national levels of responsibility for opposing correct teaching.

Key Passages

"The vessel here stands for our bodies and minds. Our minds are a kind of vessel, and our mouths too are vessels, as are our ears. The Lotus Sutra is the Dharma water of the Buddha's wisdom. But when this water is poured into our minds, then we may jar and upset it."

Nichiren uses the practical image of containers to explain how we receive and hold Buddhist wisdom. Just as physical vessels can be overturned, leak, become contaminated, or have their contents mixed with impurities, our minds can reject teachings, lose faith over time, become corrupted by negative influences, or mix the pure teaching with inferior practices. This challenges us to examine the quality and consistency of our faith.

"Or we may be the kind of practitioners of the Lotus Sutra whose mouths are reciting Nam-myoho-renge-kyo one moment, but Namu-Amida-butsu the next. This is like mixing filth with one's rice, or putting sand or pebbles in it."

Nichiren strongly criticizes the common practice of mixing different Buddhist teachings together. He argues that switching between chanting Nam-myoho-renge-kyo (the title of the Lotus Sutra) and Namu-Amida-butsu (invoking Amida Buddha) corrupts the pure practice, like contaminating good food with garbage. This underscores the importance of consistent, focused practice rather than a pick-and-choose approach to spirituality.

"Among all these men, Nichiren alone deserves to be regarded as the foremost. In what sense is he the foremost? He is foremost in being hated by men and women."

With remarkable honesty and even humor, Nichiren acknowledges that his uncompromising stance against popular religious practices has made him the most hated person in Japan. Rather than being discouraged by this, he sees it as proof that he's teaching the correct path. Standing up for truth often brings opposition, but that doesn't mean you should back down from what's right.

"The reason you have not succeeded in attaining Buddhahood from countless distant kalpas in the past down to the present is that when a situation such as this has arisen you have been too fearful to speak out."

Nichiren makes a bold claim that our spiritual progress has been blocked throughout countless lifetimes because we've been too afraid to speak up for correct principles when it mattered. He's saying that courage, especially the courage to defend truth even when it's unpopular, is essential for spiritual growth. Fear of social consequences keeps us spiritually stagnant.

"Taking faith in the Lotus Sutra is even more difficult than this... On Mount T'ien-t'ai there is a place called the Dragon Gate, which is a waterfall a thousand feet in height... If there is one fish in a hundred or a thousand that succeeds in ascending the waterfall, it will become a dragon."

Using the famous legend of fish becoming dragons by swimming up an impossible waterfall, Nichiren illustrates how rare and difficult it is to maintain true faith. Most people give up when faced with the spiritual equivalent of that rushing current: criticism, doubt, hardship, or social pressure. But for those few who persist, the transformation is as dramatic as a fish becoming a dragon.

What This Writing Is Really Saying

Nichiren's central teaching here is about the quality and purity of faith, using the metaphor of vessels to show how we can either properly receive and maintain Buddhist wisdom or lose it through four common mistakes. Just as a good container must not overturn, leak, become contaminated, or have its contents mixed with inferior substances, our faith must remain steady, consistent, pure, and undiluted by lesser teachings.

The letter then shifts to Nichiren's bold declaration that he alone stands for the correct teaching in Japan, which has made him universally hated. He explains this isn't accidental-when the entire society has accepted inferior spiritual practices, the person who points this out will naturally be despised. But this opposition proves the teaching's authenticity, not its falsehood.

Nichiren introduces his key teaching about three levels of responsibility regarding slander of correct teaching: individual slanderers who oppose it directly, families of slanderers who enable it, and countries where slander is widespread. He argues that even if you don't personally attack the correct teaching, you share responsibility if your family members or nation do so and you remain silent. This creates an obligation to speak out, even when it brings persecution.

The letter concludes with the famous Dragon Gate metaphor, explaining that achieving true faith is like a fish swimming up an impossible waterfall to become a dragon. It's extremely rare and difficult, but life-changing for those who succeed. Most people give up when faced with the rushing current of social pressure, doubt, and hardship, but the few who persist achieve something extraordinary.

How This Applies to Your Life Today

The 'four vessel faults' teach us to examine our own approach to important principles in life. Do we 'overturn' good advice by rejecting it immediately? Do we 'leak' by being inconsistent-enthusiastic about our values one day but abandoning them when tested? Do we become 'contaminated' by letting negative influences corrupt our better judgment? Do we 'mix' by trying to hold contradictory principles simultaneously? In how you eat, how you work, how you treat people - this framework helps us maintain integrity and consistency.

Nichiren's teaching about speaking out despite opposition is especially important today. Maybe it's addressing workplace misconduct, standing up to family dysfunction, or advocating for important social issues, we often face the choice between comfortable silence and difficult truth-telling. His message is that our spiritual and personal growth depends on finding the courage to voice our authentic convictions, even when it makes us unpopular. The Dragon Gate metaphor reminds us that meaningful achievement-whether in career, relationships, or personal development-requires the rare quality of persistence through seemingly impossible challenges.
